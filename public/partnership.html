The Problem

Virtual staging is a commodity. Design intelligence is not.

There are dozens of AI staging tools. They all do the same thing: pick a style label — "modern," "farmhouse," "minimalist" — and fill a room with generic furniture. None of them understand why a space works.

Room doesn't start with style labels. It starts with spatial analysis grounded in design theory — reading the room's bones, light, proportions, and character before proposing a single piece of furniture.

The Foundation

Five frameworks. Two decades of research.

Every Room design is generated through the lens of five academic design frameworks, drawn from a research corpus spanning architectural theory, environmental psychology, and universal design principles.

1
Aesthetic Order & Simplicity

Architectural integrity over superficial decoration. Harmony, balance, proportion. Sacrifice the showy for the essential. Rooted in Wharton & Codman's foundational principles.

2
Human-Centric & Functional

Anthropometry, ergonomics, proxemics. Designing for how bodies actually move through space — clearance for the 95th percentile, reach for the 5th. Respecting intimate, personal, and social distance zones.

3
Universal & Inclusive Design

The seven principles. Equitable use, perceptible information, low physical effort. Contrast and lighting that define edges. Space that works for everyone, not just the ideal case.

4
Biophilic & Regenerative

Light pools over flat illumination. Organic forms to soften and reduce stress. Prospect and refuge — balancing openness with enclosure. Materials aligned with circularity.

5
Phenomenological Principles

Genius Loci — the spirit of place. Preserving identity, orientation, light quality, structural bones. Multi-sensory awareness: acoustics, texture, haptics. Never applying generic templates that erase context.

These aren't marketing labels. They're the same frameworks taught in graduate design programs. Each design direction Room generates uses 2-3 of these frameworks, creating proposals that are intellectually defensible, not just visually pleasant.

The Lineage

Design theory has a 130-year problem.

In 1897, Edith Wharton and Ogden Codman published The Decoration of Houses — the first serious argument that interior design should follow architectural principles, not fashionable whims. Their thesis: a room should be designed from the walls inward, not the furniture outward.

A century later, we have AI tools that do exactly what Wharton warned against — picking furniture from a style catalogue and dropping it into a room with no regard for proportion, light, or spatial logic.

Room was built to close that gap. Every design direction begins with a spatial reading — understanding the room's bones before proposing a single object. The same methodology taught in graduate design programs, executed at machine speed.

From rhetoric to rooms

The builder of Room, Dustin York, studied Communication Design at NSCAD University under Hanno Ehses — a pioneer of applying rhetorical theory to visual communication. The same framework that analyzes how a poster persuades can analyze how a room communicates: what it says about the people who live there, what it invites, what it discourages.

This isn't a novel application. It's a natural extension of a 2,500-year-old tradition — from Aristotle's modes of persuasion to the spatial rhetoric of a well-designed home.

The Intelligence

Design preference data that doesn't exist anywhere else.

Every interaction generates anonymized insight: which rooms buyers engage with most, what styles they choose, what they keep versus change, how long they spend.

No one else has this data. Zillow knows what people search for. Redfin knows what people click. Room knows what people want to do with the space.

What this means for designers

Imagine knowing, before your first client meeting, that buyers in Pacific Heights overwhelmingly prefer warm organic palettes in kitchens but stark minimalism in bathrooms. That they keep hardwood 4:1 over replacing it. That open shelving gets removed in 70% of redesigns.

This is market intelligence for design professionals — derived from actual buyer behavior, not surveys or trend forecasts.
